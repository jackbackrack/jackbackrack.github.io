JBIO

by John Schoneboom

Jonathan Bachrach is an anatomically modern human with physical agility, acute senses, teeth that can cut as well as chew, and a Ph.D. in artificial intelligence. His bipedal gait is admittedly ungainly; in terms of the energy required to move proportional units of body mass over given distances, Dr. Bachrach is only slightly more efficient than a penguin. However, his locomotive adaptation provides other benefits that more than justify the bold evolutionary move. Primarily, it opens up rich new food supply niches that his four-legged cousins are unable to exploit. In short, Dr. Bachrach's upright stance frees him for a nomadic existence. While he can also flourish in place as long as resources remain abundant, when environmental circumstances change he is easily able to adapt--even to innovate.

Jonathan Bachrach's forthright bipedalism keys his success in other ways as well. Significantly, it results in a lower percentage of his body being exposed to the direct rays of the sun. In itself, this adaptation improves his ability to forage; combined with the naked skin that marks his highly efficient cooling system, Dr. Bachrach is able to withstand conditions on the open savanna that would leave other mammals hunched over and gasping in short order. In a world where sufficient hydration spells the difference between life and death, Dr. Bachrach's strategy truly gives him a leg up on the competition.

Dr. Bachrach has also been able to use his free hands to make tools. Indeed, anthropologists often consider this ability to be his foremost characteristic, calling him "Bachrach the Toolmaker." Taken together, his stance, his hands, and his cooling system have created the necessary conditions for the development of Dr. Bachrach's large cranial capacity, which currently stands at a robust 1,900 cubic centimeters.

It is difficult to overestimate the importance of brain size to Dr. Bachrach's achievements. He has learned to eat nutritious foods that eliminate the necessity of a large belly. He has formed elaborate social networks and accompanying behavioral characteristics that enrich as well as enable his survival. Most importantly of all, while he retains an atavistic flair for grooming, he relies mainly on language to cement social bonds, cooperate with others, and generate increasingly sophisticated ideas about the physical and spiritual world. It is his special preoccupation with language that has led Dr. Bachrach to work on Dylan, representing perhaps humanity's highest and most powerful achievement in the field to date.

Dr. Bachrach attributes his overall success to three fundamental preconditions: eccentricity, tilt, and the precession of the equinoxes. What he is most proud of, however, is the length of his "plumbing": Laid end to end, his blood vessels, lymph and urinary tracts would encircle the globe--twice!
